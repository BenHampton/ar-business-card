01

The mental model

Before a line of code, understand what's actually happening — this is what separates copy-paste from comprehension, and it's the first thing a senior nails down.

Marker-based AR is computer vision, not magic. Every video frame from your camera goes through this pipeline:

  1. Capture — grab a frame from the camera stream via getUserMedia.
  2. Detect — scan the frame for a known pattern: a black-bordered square, or a set of pre-computed feature points from your own image.
  3. Estimate pose — from the detected shape's distortion, solve for where the camera is relative to the marker: position and rotation, six degrees of freedom.
  4. Render — draw 3D content into that solved coordinate space, composited over the video.

All four run 30–60 times a second, in JavaScript, on a phone. Everything that goes wrong in this project traces back to one of those steps failing — and knowing which one is most of the debugging.

The tracker sees grayscale gradients, not your design. Detection works by matching high-contrast, non-repeating detail. That single fact drives every decision later: why your card's artwork matters as much as your code, why a minimalist design tracks terribly, and why "it flickers" is usually an art problem wearing an engineering costume.

The two marker families

TypeWhat it isTrade-off
Preset / barcodePre-trained patterns (Hiro, Kanji) or encoded squaresRock-solid tracking, ugly. Ships with the library.
NFTNatural Feature Tracking — your image becomes the markerLooks like a real product; tracking quality depends entirely on the artwork.

We use both: Hiro to prove the pipeline works, NFT for the actual card. That order is deliberate — validate the machinery before investing in content.

02

Project setup

We're building this as a production prototype from the first commit: real dependency management, TypeScript, a bundler, and a deploy pipeline. Not because a single AR page needs a build — it doesn't — but because this is a repo you'll show people, and the difference between "followed a tutorial" and "shipped a thing" is mostly this step.

You can defer all of this

Every bit of build machinery here — Vite, TypeScript, the vendoring plugin — is optional to getting AR working. If you'd rather see tracking first and add rigor later, the fastest path is a single HTML file with A-Frame and AR.js loaded from a CDN (pinned to the documented pair below), no build at all. The tradeoff is real and worth naming: the build-first route front-loads tooling friction (base paths, module resolution, vendoring) that has nothing to do with AR, while the CDN route gets you to a tracking marker in minutes and defers the "make it a real repo" work to when it actually matters — deploy time. Neither is wrong. This tutorial takes the build-first path; if you get stuck on plumbing, drop to CDN scripts, confirm the AR works, then reintroduce the build.

Two structural rules that will bite you later

1. Runtime assets live in public/. Vite hashes and bundles anything it imports, but copies public/ byte-for-byte. Your descriptor files are referenced by basename (url="assets/card" resolves card.fset, card.fset3, card.iset) — hashing would break that resolution silently. Put them anywhere else and you get a 404 that presents as "AR just doesn't work." (And note for Step 05: AR.js ignores Vite's base when resolving that URL, so under a base path you must prefix it yourself — covered there.)

2. Source artwork is not a runtime asset. card.png lives in design/, outside public/. The browser never loads it; it's the input to the descriptor generator and the file you send to a printer. Shipping it would waste bandwidth on every visitor.

There is no vendor/ folder in your project — on purpose

Don't go looking for it in the file tree; vendor/… in those script tags is a URL, not a directory you created. In dev, the copy plugin answers requests to it straight from node_modules; at build time it writes real files into dist/vendor/. It's the same pattern as url="assets/card" later: the served site's layout and the repo's layout are different things, and the build config is the translation between them. If a vendor/ request 404s, the plugin config in vite.config.ts is the first place to look.

Gotcha — the camera requires a secure context

getUserMedia only works on HTTPS or localhost. Opening the LAN URL (http://192.168.x.x:5173) on your phone gives you a black screen and no obvious error — the API simply isn't there. Step 04 solves this properly.

Point it at the marker

The Hiro marker is a black-bordered square with "Hiro" inside. Get the official file from the AR.js repo, then either display it on a second screen or print it. Point your phone's camera at it and a cyan cube should snap onto the pattern and track as you move.

Physical setup, spelled out

The device running the page must see the marker somewhere in the real world — a laptop webcam cannot see its own display. Phone → marker on your laptop screen, or phone → printout. Keep the whole black border in frame, avoid glare, and give it decent light.

This step is a load-bearing diagnostic, not a formality. When your own marker fails later — and it will — the question "does Hiro still work?" instantly splits the problem in half: pipeline vs artwork. Keep this marker block around, commented out, for the rest of the project. The engineers who debug fastest are the ones who kept a known-good control.

04

HTTPS, because the camera demands it

You can't test AR without a secure context, and you can't test it meaningfully on a laptop webcam. So HTTPS-to-your-phone isn't a deployment concern you defer to the end — it's step four, because every subsequent step needs it.

Option A — a trusted local certificate (best DX)

Vite can serve HTTPS directly. mkcert generates a certificate your devices actually trust, so no warning interstitials:

terminalnpm i -D vite-plugin-mkcert
vite.config.tsimport { defineConfig } from 'vite';
import { viteStaticCopy } from 'vite-plugin-static-copy';
import mkcert from 'vite-plugin-mkcert';

export default defineConfig({
  base: '/ar-business-card/',
  build: { target: 'es2020' },
  server: { host: true },   // https + LAN = your phone can load it
  plugins: [
    mkcert(),
    viteStaticCopy({ /* same targets as Step 02 — keep them */ }),
  ],
});

Now npm run dev serves https://192.168.x.x:5173 and your phone gets a camera prompt instead of a black screen.

Option B — a tunnel

terminalnpx ngrok http 5173
Two ngrok gotchas

Spelling: the package is ngrok — a typo like ngrox gives an npm E404. Interstitial: the free tier shows a warning page before your site. Tap Visit Site; the camera prompt comes after. Your AR isn't broken.

Also note the URL changes every restart, so leave that terminal alone once your phone has it.

Save-refresh, not hot reload. Vite gives you HMR, and it works fine for CSS and TS. But any change that re-initialises the scene means re-downloading and re-parsing your NFT descriptors — several seconds of nothing, every save. Many people prefer manual refresh here precisely so they control when that cost is paid.

05

Your card as the marker (NFT)

Hiro proved the pipeline. Now the real thing: your own artwork as the tracking target. This is where most projects quietly fail, and the failure looks like a code bug but almost never is one.

How NFT actually works

The name is unfortunate — NFT here means Natural Feature Tracking, nothing to do with crypto. Hiro works because its pattern is pre-trained into AR.js. Your card isn't, and analysing an image from scratch is far too expensive to do 30 times a second on a phone.

So the analysis happens once, offline. A generator scans your artwork for distinctive feature points — corners, edges, high-contrast detail — records them at multiple scales, and writes three descriptor files. At runtime AR.js matches the camera feed against that pre-computed fingerprint, which is cheap. That's the whole trick.

The descriptors are only as good as the artwork. The generator can't extract features that don't exist. A minimalist card with generous whitespace produces a sparse fingerprint, which produces jittery tracking that drops constantly — and no amount of code fixes it. Design the card for the tracker, or accept flicker. This is the single highest-leverage decision in the project.

Designing artwork that tracks

  • No large flat regions. Solid colour blocks and empty whitespace are feature deserts. Texture must reach into every zone, however subtly.
  • No repeating patterns. A uniform grid gives thousands of features that all look identical — the tracker can't tell top-left from bottom-right, and the lock slides. The test: crop any 15% of the card at random. Could you tell where it came from? If not, neither can the tracker.
  • 300+ DPI source. For a 3.5×2″ card that's ≥1050×600px. Low-DPI art forces users to hold the phone unnaturally close and still.
  • Flat RGB, no alpha. Generate descriptors from the trimmed art — not the bleed version, since bleed isn't visible on the printed card.
  • A QR code is your friend. Dense, unique, genuinely useful — the best tracking feature you can legitimately put on a business card.

Generating the descriptors

Use the NFT Marker Creator. (Note: the AR.js-org page at a similar URL only shows the README — no upload button.) Click Upload Image and check the confidence stars before generating: aim for 3+. That rating is a direct verdict on your artwork, and it's cheaper to heed it now than after you've printed cards.

The bug that looks exactly like a broken tracker

Under a base path, a bare url="assets/card" 404s — silently. With base: '/ar-business-card/', the descriptor request must go to /ar-business-card/assets/card.fset, but AR.js resolves assets/card against the site origin, asking for /assets/card.fset — a 404. Worse, AR.js's NFT worker swallows the failure in a bare .catch that only console.logs it: arjs-nft-loaded never fires, your loader never clears, and 20 seconds later you get the timeout. The camera runs the whole time (video is independent of the tracker), so it presents as a tracking or artwork problem when the descriptors were never fetched. This single bug can eat an evening.

Prefix the base yourself. Vite exposes it as import.meta.env.BASE_URL, so the robust fix is to set the URL from script rather than hardcode it — that survives a repo rename or a move to a custom domain:

src/main.ts (addition, inside the else-branch)// AR.js resolves <a-nft url> against the origin, ignoring Vite's base — so
// build the full path ourselves. BASE_URL is '/' in dev, '/ar-business-card/'
// in the deployed build; either way this resolves correctly.
const nft = document.querySelector('a-nft');
nft?.setAttribute('url', `${import.meta.env.BASE_URL}assets/card`);
Attribute casing bites here too

Smooth options must be kebab-case. HTML lowercases attributes, and the a-nft primitive registers smooth-count, smooth-tolerance, smooth-threshold. Write them camelCase (smoothCount) and the browser hands AR.js smoothcount, which maps to nothing — the option is silently ignored and you get default jitter with no error.

url is a basename, not a path. assets/card resolves the three card.* files. If your generator named them after the source image, rename all three or update the URL — and remember hosting is case-sensitive.

Descriptors take seconds to load

Unlike Hiro, NFT descriptors are downloaded and parsed at startup. Nothing can track before that finishes. AR.js fires arjs-nft-loaded on window — note the asymmetry, marker events fire on the scene but this one doesn't. Step 06 turns this into a real loading state; for now, wire a signal so you know when it's safe to point at the card.

A note on emitevents

Older tutorials (and earlier drafts of this one) tell you to set emitevents="true" or events won't fire. In current AR.js (3.4.x) that attribute is effectively a no-op — the tracking events fire and bubble up to the <a-scene> regardless, which is why the code in Step 06 listens on the scene. Harmless to include, but don't count on it as the fix if events go missing; check that you're listening on the scene, not the marker node, and that your descriptors actually loaded. Behaviour like this shifts across AR.js versions — trust your running build over any tutorial's claim, this one included.

06

The application layer, in TypeScript

Here's where the setup from Step 02 starts paying. AR code fails in a specific, nasty way: silently, on a phone, with no console open. TypeScript converts a whole class of those into compile errors.

Where types help — and where they stop

Be honest about the boundary. A-Frame's attributes are runtime-parsed strings (animation="property: rotation; to: -90 360 0"), opaque to any type system. @types/aframe covers the JS API but not custom elements, and AR.js's events aren't in any event map. So: type your code, and hand-declare the AR surface you touch. About twenty lines, and writing them forces you to name your assumptions instead of scattering as any.

types/ar.d.ts// AR.js emits these on the scene element. They're in no standard event map,
// so without this declaration `markerFound` is just an untyped string and a
// typo like `makerLost` compiles happily and never fires.
interface ARSceneEventMap extends HTMLElementEventMap {
  'markerFound': CustomEvent;
  'markerLost': CustomEvent;
  'arjs-video-loaded': CustomEvent;
}

export interface ARScene extends HTMLElement {
  addEventListener<K extends keyof ARSceneEventMap>(
    type: K,
    listener: (ev: ARSceneEventMap[K]) => void
  ): void;
}

// Fired on window, NOT the scene — a real asymmetry in AR.js.
// Encoding it here means you can't get it wrong at the call site.
declare global {
  interface WindowEventMap {
    'arjs-nft-loaded': CustomEvent;
  }
}

Tracking state

src/tracking.tsimport type { ARScene } from '../types/ar';

export function initTracking(scene: ARScene, hud: HTMLElement): void {
  // Events bubble to the scene — listen there, not on the marker node.
  // This survives swapping <a-nft> for <a-marker> and back.
  scene.addEventListener('markerFound', () => {
    hud.style.display = 'none';
  });

  scene.addEventListener('markerLost', () => {
    hud.textContent = 'Card lost — point back at it';
    hud.style.display = 'block';
  });
}

Camera guards

src/camera.tsimport type { ARScene } from '../types/ar';

export function showFatal(msg: string): void {
  // Inline styles deliberately: this runs AFTER wiping the DOM, and it's
  // exactly the moment you can't assume the stylesheet loaded.
  document.body.innerHTML =
    `<div style="position:fixed; inset:0; display:flex;
      align-items:center; justify-content:center; padding:24px;
      background:#0a0d0f; color:#fff; text-align:center;
      font-family:system-ui,sans-serif; font-size:18px;
      line-height:1.5;">${msg}</div>`;
}

export function guardCamera(scene: ARScene): void {
  scene.addEventListener('arjs-video-loaded', () => {
    console.info('Camera stream OK');
  });

  // TWO distinct failures. Feature detection only catches the first one,
  // and the second is far more common.
  if (!navigator.mediaDevices?.getUserMedia) {
    showFatal("This browser can't access the camera. Try Chrome or Safari.");
    return;
  }

  // Probe it ourselves. Browsers cache the grant for the session, so AR.js's
  // own request typically reuses it without a second prompt — behaviour
  // varies slightly by browser, but a double prompt is harmless.
  navigator.mediaDevices.getUserMedia({ video: true })
    .then((stream) => stream.getTracks().forEach((t) => t.stop()))
    .catch((err: DOMException) => {
      if (err.name === 'NotAllowedError') {
        showFatal('Camera access was denied. Allow camera permission in ' +
                  'your browser settings, then reload.');
      } else {
        showFatal(`No usable camera found (${err.name}).`);
      }
    });
}

Wiring it together

src/main.tsimport './styles.css';
import type { ARScene } from '../types/ar';
import { initTracking } from './tracking';
import { guardCamera, showFatal } from './camera';

// Loud failure beats silent failure. If the vendor scripts didn't load —
// wrong path, broken copy config, bad deploy — every downstream symptom is
// just "AR doesn't work". Assert once, at the top, and say so.
if (!('AFRAME' in window)) {
  showFatal('AR libraries failed to load.');
  throw new Error('AFRAME missing — check the vendor scripts / copy plugin');
}

const scene = document.querySelector('a-scene') as ARScene | null;
const hud = document.querySelector<HTMLElement>('#hud');
const loader = document.querySelector<HTMLElement>('#loader');

if (!scene || !hud) {
  showFatal('Page failed to initialise.');
} else {
  guardCamera(scene);
  initTracking(scene, hud);

  // Descriptors are several seconds of nothing on mobile. Say so.
  window.addEventListener('arjs-nft-loaded', () => loader?.remove());

  // If they never load, don't leave a spinner forever.
  setTimeout(() => {
    if (document.body.contains(loader)) {
      showFatal('Could not load tracking data. Check your connection and reload.');
    }
  }, 20_000);
}

Design every state, not just the happy path. This scene has four: loading descriptors, searching for the marker, tracking, and lost. A user pointing a phone at a card with no feedback assumes it's broken within about three seconds. The HUD isn't decoration — it's the difference between "this doesn't work" and "I need to move closer."

Why distinct states are the point of this step

If #loader and #hud render as identical unstyled black text stacked in a corner, a stuck loader looks exactly like the tracking prompt. You'll read "Loading…" as "Point your camera at the card," conclude the descriptors loaded fine, and go hunting for a tracking or artwork problem that doesn't exist — while the real fault (a swallowed descriptor 404, say) sits upstream. Two colours and two screen positions convert that invisible ambiguity into a glance: purple-at-top means descriptors still haven't loaded, and if it never turns off, your bug is loading, not tracking. Design the states before you need to debug them.

07

Real 3D content

A cyan box proves tracking. A business card needs something worth showing. We'll load a .glb — the single-file, compressed, web-standard 3D format.

Where to get a model

Download: Poly Pizza (free, low-poly, glTF-ready — low-poly is exactly what mobile AR wants) or Sketchfab (check file size; photogrammetry models run 50 MB+). Generate: see Step 11. Model it: if your logo is already vector, importing the SVG into Blender and extruding it takes minutes and the result is unambiguously yours.

Prefer single-file .glb over .gltf, which references separate texture and .bin files you'd also have to copy — the number one "model won't load" cause. And check the licence: most Poly Pizza models are CC-BY, meaning free use with a credit line.

      Scale is a hunt, not a value
      
Every model has its own native size — one artist's "1 unit" is a centimetre, another's is a kilometre. Start at scale="1" and judge from there: an AI-generated or CC model is often already 100–200 units, which reads as a sensible fraction of the 1050-pixel-wide card. If it's a giant that swallows the camera (you see nothing, or a flat wall of colour — that's a backface from inside the mesh), sweep down (1 → 0.5 → 0.1). If it's a speck, sweep up. Don't blindly reach for 100 — on a model that's already 150 units native, that's a 15,000-unit skyscraper and the classic "tracking works but nothing shows." Same care for rotation: start at 0 0 0 to see it, then -90 0 0 lays a logo flat against the card; a thin sliver usually means wrong rotation, not wrong scale.

Making it fast

Your visitor is standing in a hallway on cellular data, holding a phone at a card, waiting. Everything below is measured against that.

Compress the model

The single biggest win. Draco compression routinely takes a 5 MB .glb to under 500 KB with no visible difference at card scale:

terminalnpm i -D @gltf-transform/cli

# Don't overwrite your only copy. The uncompressed original is a SOURCE
# asset — same doctrine as the card artwork — so it lives in design/,
# and the Draco pass emits the runtime copy into public/.
mv public/assets/card-model.glb design/card-model-src.glb
npx gltf-transform optimize design/card-model-src.glb \
    public/assets/card-model.glb --compress draco
Draco needs a decoder

Compressed models require A-Frame's Draco decoder path to be configured, or the model silently fails to load. Set <a-scene gltf-model="dracoDecoderPath: https://www.gstatic.com/draco/v1/decoders/"> — and yes, that's a CDN dependency you should vendor if you're being strict.

The descriptor cost

Your three card.* files can total several MB, and they're on the critical path — nothing tracks until they're parsed. Serve them gzipped (most static hosts do this automatically; verify rather than assume) and set long cache headers, since they only change when your artwork does.

A tunnel URL dies when you close the terminal. A portfolio piece needs a permanent address — and your card's QR code needs somewhere to point that still exists next year.

GitHub Pages gives you free HTTPS (mandatory for the camera) and a stable URL. Push to main, and the build runs and deploys:

The base-path trap

Project sites live at https://<user>.github.io/<REPO>/, so base in vite.config.ts must be '/<REPO>/'. Get it wrong and every asset 404s — including your descriptors, which surfaces not as an error but as "the AR just doesn't work." Use '/' only for a custom domain or a <user>.github.io repo.

Close the loop: the QR code

Now that the URL is permanent, generate a QR code pointing at it and place it in the card artwork — this is the moment the product actually comes together. It's also, per Step 05, the densest tracking feature you can add. One consequence people miss: changing the artwork invalidates the descriptors. Re-run the NFT Marker Creator on the final art (QR included), replace all three card.* files, and re-test before sending anything to a printer.

Other hosts

Netlify, Vercel, and Cloudflare Pages all detect Vite automatically, deploy on push, and give you preview URLs per branch. Any of them is a defensible choice; Pages wins on "it's already where your code is."

Deploy before you're finished, not after. The first deploy always surfaces something local dev hid — a base path, a case-sensitive filename, a missing asset. Finding that on commit three costs ten minutes. Finding it the night before you hand out cards costs you the cards.

The repo is the product

If this is a portfolio piece, be clear-eyed about how it gets consumed: most people will read the README, look at one image, and never run the code. That's not cynicism — it's the reality your repo has to survive.

## How it works

The card isn't a QR code or a black-and-white marker — it's a *natural
feature* target. Offline, the NFT Marker Creator analyses the artwork and
extracts hundreds of distinctive feature points, saved as three descriptor
files. At runtime AR.js matches the camera feed against that fingerprint,
solves the card's position and orientation, and A-Frame renders the model
onto that pose.

This is why the card's *design* matters as much as the code: flat or
repetitive artwork yields few unique features, and tracking degrades no
matter how clean your JavaScript is.
→ [Write-up of that trade-off](docs/marker-design.md)

## Stack

A-Frame (ECS over three.js) · AR.js (NFT tracking) · TypeScript · Vite

### Why A-Frame and not React Three Fiber?

This is a single-scene static page. A-Frame's ECS maps onto it directly and
a heavier abstraction would be ceremony. Inside a React product I'd choose
R3F for shared state and type safety — here it would mean hand-wiring pose
data into three.js to reach an identical result.

### Why classic scripts + a copy plugin, not imports?

AR.js is a browser-global script, not a module — bundling it means betting
on side-effect ordering that breaks silently across library versions. The
copy plugin keeps versions pinned in package.json and the deploy
deterministic, and main.ts asserts AFRAME exists so if the mechanism ever
breaks, the failure is a message, not a blank page.

Appendix: making your own model with AI

Optional, and independent of the build — do it whenever you want to replace a downloaded model with something custom. If you don't model in 3D, AI image-to-3D closes that gap in minutes. Several tools exist (Hyper3D, Trify3D, Sloyd); this walks through Meshy, which has the most usable free tier.

What the free tier actually gives you

100 credits/month, no card required, resetting on the 1st at 00:00 UTC with no rollover. A full textured generation costs about 20 credits — roughly five models a month. Connecting a Discord account is a one-time +100, and earned credits don't expire like the monthly allowance.

Two limits worth knowing before spending credits: free accounts cannot download Meshy 6 models (paywall on the Download button) but can download up to 10 Meshy 5 models per month. Free output is licensed CC BY 4.0 — commercial use allowed with attribution. Terms change; check the pricing page.

Step by step

  1. Sign up at meshy.ai. Credits are available immediately; the workspace is where everything happens.
  2. Pick a mode. Image to 3D if you have art (a logo, a sketch); Text to 3D if you're inventing an object. Image gives far more control.
  3. Prepare the input. Image-to-3D predicts volume from flat pixels — it invents the back and sides. So: one subject, centred, filling the frame, on a plain or transparent background (there's a Remove background button on hover). Backgrounds that bleed into the subject's edge corrupt the mesh boundary — the model can't tell where your object stops. At least 1024px on the short side; PNG/JPG/WebP up to 20 MB.
  4. Choose Meshy 5, not the default. Meshy 6 is better and you can't download it on the free tier. Select this before generating or you'll spend credits on a model you can only rotate in a browser.
  5. Set Model Type to Low Poly (Topology Mesh). It generates efficient geometry rather than decimating a dense mesh. Target 500–2,000 faces.
  6. Generate, then judge the silhouette, not the surface. At business-card scale on a phone, the outline is the entire experience. Generation is stochastic; regenerating is normal and costs credits again.
  7. Texture if you want colour — a second AI pass, guided by a short prompt, and the second thing that costs credits.
  8. Export GLB, rename to card-model.glb, drop in public/assets/. Then re-run the Draco pass from Step 08 and re-hunt the scale.

When the result is wrong

  • Blobby, melted detail — under-described features get smoothed away. Name materials and details explicitly.
  • Extra limbs or heads — plural nouns overweight features. "warriors" → "warrior".
  • Unwanted asymmetry — add "symmetrical" to the prompt.
  • Floating accessories — describe the connection ("helmet on head", not "helmet").
  • Smeared surfaces — re-run texturing with a targeted prompt.
  • Just… off — regenerate. Budget credits for two or three attempts at anything you actually care about.
Attribution isn't optional

CC BY 4.0 lets you use this commercially — including on a card promoting your own work — but you must credit Meshy. The contact fallback from Step 07 and your README are the natural homes: "3D model generated with Meshy (CC BY 4.0)" satisfies it. Free-plan generations are also public, so don't feed it an unreleased logo.

Want private assets and no attribution? That's the paid tier. Want to skip the question entirely? If your logo is already vector, importing the SVG into Blender and extruding it is a few clicks, unambiguously yours, and gives exact geometry rather than an approximation.

Generated is a starting point, not a deliverable. AI 3D is an excellent ideation-to-blockout tool, and finishing by hand — cleaning topology, fixing orientation — is still normal. Judge output against what your project needs rather than a demo reel: for a spinning object at card scale, "right silhouette, under 2,000 faces" is genuinely done. For a hero asset, it isn't.
